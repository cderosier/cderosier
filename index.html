01
Who I Am

My name is Charles DeRosier IV, and I am completing my Bachelor of General Studies with an emphasis in Psychology at the Arkansas State University. I also hold an Associate of Arts in Sociology from Long Beach City College and previously pursued a double major in Political Science and Psychology at Arkansas State University — where I earned a spot on the Sun Belt Conference Academic Honor Roll.

I currently serve as a Ramp Agent for JetBlue Airlines at Los Angeles International Airport. My professional identity is built on one belief: that understanding people is the foundation of doing any job well.

02
My Academic & Professional Journey

My path to Psychology began in an Introductory Psychology course that had such a lasting impact on my academic journey that I changed my major the following semester. Psychology drew me in because it can genuinely be seen as an interdisciplinary field — overlapping with sport psychology, educational psychology, and most recently, human factors in aviation. As a ramp agent at LAX, I've found that understanding the tendencies and patterns of my coworkers directly creates a safer, more effective work environment.

Two academic experiences stand out as pivotal: completing PSY 440: Cognition, which expanded my thinking about metacognition and how we think about thinking, and my City Planning internship in Jonesboro where I worked directly with government officials, contractors, and the public. Both deepened my ability to apply psychological insight to real-world, high-stakes contexts.

03
Career Goals & Contributions

In the short term, I am focused on completing my final courses at Arkansas State with work I am genuinely proud of, while advancing within JetBlue — where I currently hold a junior lead role. I know how close I am to earning my bachelor's degree and understand the impact it will have on my ability to advance within my own workplace.

Long term, my goal is to step into human resources or a role involving human relations within the aviation or transportation industry — where my psychology knowledge, operational experience, and bilingual communication skills converge. My degree will mark not just academic achievement, but the full nonlinear journey it took to get here. I want to use everything I've learned to build workplaces where people are genuinely understood and supported.

IDS 4013 · Applied Problem-Solving Project

Mental Health &
Psychological Safety
in Aviation.

The aviation industry operates at the intersection of extreme physical demand, shift work, high-stakes safety responsibility, and often — limited psychological support. Frontline workers like ramp agents, ground crew, and cabin staff face chronic stress, fatigue, and interpersonal conflict with few formal outlets. I selected this problem because I live it every day at LAX, and my psychology emphasis gives me a unique lens to address it.

01
The Problem

Workforce mental health and psychological safety among frontline aviation workers is widely underaddressed. High turnover, safety incidents linked to human factors, and a culture that often discourages vulnerability create environments where workers are physically present but psychologically depleted — increasing risk for everyone.

02
Connection to Emphasis Areas

Psychology: Human factors in aviation, stress and cognition, team communication patterns, and burnout research all directly inform this problem. My coursework in PSY 440 and human development provides the academic foundation to analyze it rigorously.

Sociology: Organizational culture, shift-work inequality, and the structural conditions that normalize overwork in aviation are sociological problems requiring systemic — not just individual — solutions.

Political Science: FAA regulations, labor policy, and corporate HR structures govern how (or whether) mental health resources reach frontline workers. Policy literacy is essential to propose realistic change.

03
Proposed Approach

Drawing on my psychology training, aviation experience, and bilingual communication skills, I propose a peer-support and psychological safety program for frontline airport workers that includes:

→Trained peer advocates embedded within ground operations crews
→Bilingual mental health resources for Spanish-speaking workforce members
→Structured team debriefs after high-stress incidents (modeled on aviation CRM principles)
→Partnership with airline HR departments and Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs)

Potential collaborators: JetBlue HR, FAA Human Factors Division, SHRM, university psychology departments.

04
Impact Statement

A psychologically safer aviation workforce benefits everyone — workers, passengers, and airlines. Reduced burnout means fewer human-error incidents. Better communication means safer ramp operations. Inclusive, bilingual resources mean broader reach across the diverse workforce that keeps major airports like LAX running. The interdisciplinary approach — combining psychology, sociology, and policy — is precisely what makes this solution more than just a wellness initiative. It's a systems-level intervention.
